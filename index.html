About Me

I am a Ph.D. candidate in the Robotics at Georgia Institute of Technology, advised by Dr. Aaron Young in the Exoskeleton & Prosthetic Intelligent Controls (EPIC) Lab.

I develop AI-powered wearable systems to understand and improve human mobility—with a clinical foundation that keeps my work grounded in real needs and translatable to real life.

My research bridges machine learning and wearable robotics for assistive and rehabilitative applications:

  • Machine learning models for human movement understanding, deployed in real-time for closed-loop wearable robot control
  • Wearable system development—from sensing and hardware design to embedded implementation
  • Large-scale biomechanics datasets with neuromuscular modeling and simulation to advance data-driven movement science

Before robotics, I studied medicine and investigated human movement through biomechanical and neuromuscular research—how muscles compensate, how gait adapts, how the body responds to intervention. That foundation shapes how I design, build, and validate.

Research Highlights

Tuning-Free Hip Exoskeleton via Deep Learning

Developed a subject-independent deep learning model (TCN) to estimate physiological effort in real-time. This technology enables a hip exoskeleton to automatically adapt to stroke survivors without manual calibration. Clinical trials demonstrated reduced metabolic cost and improved mobility across diverse daily activities.

Learn more →

Open-Source Stroke Biomechanics Dataset

Created a comprehensive multimodal dataset capturing 20 stroke survivors performing 39 real-world activities. Provides synchronized MoCap, IMU, and EMG data alongside a Python toolbox to bridge the gap between engineering development and clinical biomechanics.

Learn more →

Human-in-the-Loop Optimization

Developed a data-efficient optimization framework that autonomously discovers personalized assistance profiles. Using Bayesian optimization with real-time feedback, the system reduced metabolic cost by 11.4% during stair climbing compared to unassisted walking.

Learn more →

Ergonomic Hardware & Soft Sensing

Developed a fully portable, lightweight (2.5 kg) hip exoskeleton integrated with a soft "Second Skin" sensing suite. This versatile platform features novel adjustment mechanisms to accommodate diverse patient physiologies and served as the core hardware for my clinical trials and dataset collection studies.
